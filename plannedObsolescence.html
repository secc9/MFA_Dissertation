Planned Obsolescence

Can you remember the last thing you owned that stopped working unexpectedly? Was it your toaster, kettle, fridge, television, computer, the car? In my case, I cannot remember what it could be, but many of my things often break. I have been told that numerous times that I am heavy handed. When purchasing new equipment, you get a standard manufacturer warranty for 1, 2, or even 5 years. But when that warranty expires, the equipment suddenly and mysteriously dies shortly after. This has been happening for as long as I can remember, and I can not be the only person witnessing this phenomenon. A friend and colleague of mine spends his time scouring internet sale sites for audio and music equipment using spares or repair filters. After 'winning' the item, he spends time repairing and using it before selling it again. I have always admired his approach. Still, my friend has an electronic engineering background and understands what he is doing. I have attempted this, only to be let down by my lack of knowledge and skills. My enthusiastic results end with more equipment that has been 'broken twice' due to my lack of knowledge and experience, an empty wallet and even more crushed self-confidence.

In his pamphlet, published in 1932, "Ending the Depression Through Planned Obsolescence", London complains about how people are using the things they own for much longer that was the norm before the depression. In this case, people are no longer buying anything new due to the great depression the US faced in the early 1930's. He writes that before the depression, "They replaced old articles with new for reasons of fashion" and "They gave up old homes and old automobiles long before they were worn out, merely because they were obsolete."1

The US great depression saw mass unemployment, decline in producion, famine, among many other things. To get a real idea of what it could be like I would reccomne It was proposed by London that the way to get out of this depression was for him to propose the following idea:

I would have the Government assign a lease of life to shoes and homes and machines, to all products of manufacture, mining and agriculture, when they are first created, and they would be sold and used with the term of their existence definitely known by the consumer. After the allotted time had expired, these things would be legally “dead” and would be controlled by the duly appointed governmental agency and destroyed if there is widespread unemployment. New products would constantly be pouring forth from the factories and marketplaces, to take the place of the obsolete, and the wheels of industry would be kept going and employment regularized and assured for the masses.2

This is the first idea of planned obsolescence. The Government should set an 'expiration date' for all products. This would assume that factories could continue to make the products as the products already existing in the world would be, at some point in the near future, coming to the end of their life. Consumers would throw out their old products and keep purchasing new ones. The need for production creates employment, therefore supplying a disposable income to consumers who could continue to buy the products that had become obsolete.

London's idea goes further and stems into the realm of government bureaucracy. Consumers should return out-of-life products to government facilities, where they will be issued some receipt telling of what goods and the state of goods was when returned, with the possibility of some kind of financial remuneration dependent on the condition of the goods. We could read into this that this act would demand an even higher level of employment. Operating this scheme would require large teams working in Government nationwide. I have, once upon a time, been a lowly local government employee, and I can already see the red tape. The endless forms to be filled in by the consumer before even making it out of the house with any items to be returned. Queues around the returns building with items ranging from a pair of scissors to a tractor. Being sent away due to filling out a form incorrectly and having some inexperienced assessor incorrectly evaluate your car or carriage clock. People fed up with the experience would eventually dump belongings on the street. Maybe a black market appears. This is something that has already been long happening in society. For the last 15 years, we have had a large skip in the car park at the rear of our home. This has long been a common dumping site for people too lazy to go to the local tip. Under the cover of darkness, people are clearing out their homes. Tradesmen empty their vans with full kitchens and bathrooms. Landscape gardeners dumping a garden's worth of vegetation.

Skip at the rear of authors flat.

London believes that the Government is, or at least could be, a well-oiled machine. My experience working for the local Government was the opposite. London's vision, in part, is in process right now, but it is failing. Factories are constantly growing, producing products at rates he could have only imagined. In London's writing, there are plenty of ideas for destroying items but no reasoning for how the items are destroyed and what is to be done with the remains. We have to remember that this is the 1930's. Little thought is given to the afterlife of an item. Return your item to a place, re-purchase a brand-new replacement and forget about it. We are there. We did make it this far. London would be pleased if he were alive to see the results. I cannot help but feel as though London is proposing the idea of a Communist state to prop up a Capitalist ideology. We are already be doing this anyway, as in the 2008 banking crisis the bankers were bailed out by the government payouts and even more recently in the Covid Pandemic.

Why shouldn't we go back to the thinking of the times of the great depression? The only people wanting us to consume more are the CEOs of corporations and the politicians who do their bidding. With the world finally catching up to full automation, London's idea of full employment in the production of planned obsolescence will be obsolete. We are no longer living in the 1930's. We are fast approaching the 2030s. We are more likely to be living through a collective depression brought on by the illusion of forced aspirational living and overconsumption of products in the pursuit of this ideal and a financial one due to the pursuit of profit. A better, more well-rounded free education system with easy access for everyone to fundamental skills would be a better solution. People could have access to teach themselves the skills to repair their items. For a while now, repair shops and hackspaces have been popping up, offering consumers the chance to repair. Still, with only a limited amount of people with the skills to repair, wait times can be long, depending on the type of repair, and the repair cafes are only every so often due to being run by volunteers and can usually only be found in larger towns and cities, so travel could be required to complete the project. For instance, according to repaircafe.org,3 the city of London has 12 cafes running, but cities such as Guildford and York have none. Smaller towns like Filey or Whitehaven are so far from anywhere with a cafe that this is almost an impossible task (without serious travel planning). How important is it to keep that kettle running?

April 2024 saw a legal overturn of the right to repair.4 The European Parliament voted for manufacturers to extend access to repairs, spare parts and repair information. This is a great start, but we, as consumers, still lack the basic skills to do it ourselves.

As I sit in my 'studio' room writing this, I have two plumbers sent from my landlord to replace the failing bathroom tiling and, unfortunately, replace the original cast iron bath, which has been in situ since the flat was built sometime in the 60s. My partner and I quickly discussed whether the old bath could still be used at our allotment as a planter for garlic (since we recently destroyed our plantation from onion white rot). Still, we concluded that we did not have enough space. I can't help feeling as though we are missing out on something. A chance to keep something alive for just a little longer. To give a new life to a repurposed item that has already worked hard. As long as this is an inanimate object. But to expect this behaviour from a living being would be unacceptable. Working animals have a lifespan before being put out to pasture. We, humans, have a retirement age (although this is constantly creeping up as each successive Government decides they need more workforce or can't afford to give out pensions. Death by stealth is a neat way of cutting costs). Only after this do we find ourselves finally taking up that hobby of tinkering that we put off our entire working lives.
