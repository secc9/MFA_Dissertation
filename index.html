Bringing the dead back to life.
Attempting to save affordable and disposable music controllers from oblivion.


Introduction

This dissertation explores the act of creating and distributing open source audio tools, saving and reusing cheap or obsolete music midi controllers 1 and the politics surrounding it. This simple notion of open source is an act of resistance and rebellion in the face of post-capitalist structures, labour forces and the industry complex inherent in these constructs. By eschewing the now traditional (read as prescribed, forced, given) routes of digital artistic production, we can hopefully find that the users of these technologies can travel along another path, albeit one much less well-trodden, but all the more fulfilling because of that fact. When beginning to travel this path, the user will be empty-handed and almost naked. However, along the way, they will slowly find themselves becoming equipped with a multitude of tools and armour that will help them hack away and protect themselves from the jungle in front of them and, in time, emerge at the foot of a great clearing, where they can construct their village or town, much like Macondo2. Here the inhabitants are free (for the time being) to create and live how they wish without the interference from external power.

The music equipment industry is much like any other industry. One built on the ideology of capitalism. Larger scale companies even more so. Releasing new technology each cycle, advertised to make the user believe that this piece of equipment will be the key piece that will finally unlock and unleash the creativity hidden deep inside the creator's unconsciousness. Maybe we aren't the problem? Maybe the huge amount of propaganda3 we are force-fed on a daily basis is? Maybe new equipment isn't the answer? Maybe the answer lies the tools which we are already working with. Maybe it's something more than that? Equipment is usually expensive to purchase, and once it stops working. Then what? Hopefully, sell it. Pop it into the recycling bin and continue adding it to landfills? Or maybe just throw it in the river and be done with it.

The first of these solutions is a valid thing as I have done it myself, selling to a high street chain that takes belongings without much testing or questioning, whilst hoping to, but never receiving a reasonable remuneration. What about the opposite of this? Why not purchase something affordable at the cost of maybe four or five coffees from a popular, hip coffee shop? Who cares if it has been used? Who cares if the casing is battered? What does it matter if some of the functions are broken? Maybe the item has some interesting backstory that the new owner would never know about. Maybe there is some inherent Vudu deep within the piece that would change outcomes and outputs for the new owner and send them spiralling in a new direction? What matters is being able to access hardware and use it with some software for a reasonable cost without feeling the overwhelming burden of having to do something with it, or even stick with it for the forseeable future. Gone are the days of synthesizers costing more than a down payment on a mortgage. Access to cheap, used machines is here. We, the consumers, just need to break free of our chains and the stranglehold the propaganda machine has on us.

I am researching access to technology. Things that can limit the entrance to making music/sound. Historically, access to Music and the arts has been for the more privileged, and the lower classes are made to be happy with their lot. The lower classes manufacture the instruments, and the upper classes use them and produce work to be displayed at functions and concerts that the lower classes cannot afford. Is it possible to purchase obsolete music controller equipment and write software for it? So what? Why do this? I started this path almost 17 years ago. looking at a way to make my own instruments using coding languages and the long, eventual break away from traditional software has opened new doors I would have never known about if I hadn’t started on this path. How obsolete is obsolete? When will the actual equipment no longer be able to work or maybe even be supported? My idea isn’t a new idea by any means. Developing countries have long been grafting old components together for repairs due to a lack of access. Even here in the more upwardly mobile Western countries, there is still a place for vintage guitar and studio equipment, even if there’s a lack of people with the skills to repair them. The human labour costs for these products are high. All the people involved in making and even selling a product. This is the labour I'm interested in. The number of people who have touched a product before a purchase can be high. From mining raw materials to factory workers turning materials into components, hardware design, electronics design, software design, production build, documentation, marketing, and retail. What about the human cost of these? Workers working long hours doing repetitive labour to make something they cannot afford. Forgotten technology. Consumer cycles and capitalism’s model of growth demand new consumption. The question of accessibility and affordability can almost go hand in hand Not everyone has access to modern technology. Even access to a computer is lower than expected. xolAccording to the Digital Poverty Alliance, 26% of young people do not have access to a laptop or similar device. It can be difficult to purchase things that can bring joy when there is little spare money at the end of the week/month, especially at this time when everyone is finding themselves stretched to the brink financially. Reuse and recycling can bring old equipment back to life and be a small act of rebellion against the current pollution, climate and political crisis. "That when you throw something out, there's not "out". Rather there's a human being who has to lift it, haul it, get injured because of it, dispose of it".
